When cost flows both ways

Support functions don't just serve the front line — they serve each other. IT keeps HR running; HR staffs IT. Reciprocal apportionment is how you allocate shared cost fairly when the arrows point both directions at once.

Origins → practice Worked example inside ~10 min read
01 — The problem

A chicken-and-egg in the cost ledger

Allocating overhead is easy when cost flows one way. A canteen serves the factory floor; you split the canteen's cost across the floor by headcount and you're done. The trouble starts when service departments serve one another.

Say Maintenance keeps IT's servers cool and IT runs Maintenance's scheduling system. To allocate Maintenance's cost properly, you need to load in IT's share first — but IT's cost isn't final either, because it's still owed a share of Maintenance. Finish one and you've understated the other. It's circular by nature, and pretending it isn't produces numbers that don't reconcile.

This is the reciprocal-services problem, and it shows up any time shared functions depend on each other — which, in a real organisation, is most of the time.

The reciprocal method is the only one that answers the circular question exactly, rather than cutting the loop and hoping the error is small.
02 — Three approaches

Three ways to cut the knot

Cost accounting offers three methods for allocating service-department cost. They differ in one thing only: which of the arrows between service departments they're willing to draw.

03 — A worked example

Watch the methods disagree

Two service departments — Maintenance (A) at $90,000 and IT (B) at $25,000 — and two production lines, P1 and P2. Each service department splits its effort like so:

  • Maintenance (A) works 25% for IT, 45% for P1, 30% for P2.
  • IT (B) works 20% for Maintenance, 30% for P1, 50% for P2.

The reciprocal method treats each department's true cost as its own spend plus whatever the other loads onto it. Two unknowns, two equations:

A = $90,000 + 0.20 · B
B = $25,000 + 0.25 · A
──────────────────────────
solve together →  A = $100,000,  B = $50,000

Those reciprocated totals are larger than the original spend — $100k and $50k against $90k and $25k — because each now carries the cost flowing back from the other. Allocate those totals outward and the real cost of each production line falls out. Run the same data through all three methods and the answers pull apart:

MethodP1P2Total
Direct$63,375.00$51,625.00$115,000
Step-down (A first)$58,312.50$56,687.50$115,000
Reciprocal$60,000.00$55,000.00$115,000

Every method returns the same grand total — no cost is lost — but only the reciprocal figure is order-independent and internally consistent. Change the step-down order (settle IT first) and its split moves again; the reciprocal answer never does.

04 — Origins

Borrowed from how economies feed themselves

The maths that solves reciprocal cost allocation wasn't invented for accountants. It came from economics — specifically from Wassily Leontief's input-output analysis, which he introduced in 1941 to model how the industries of a whole economy consume one another's output. Steel needs coal; coal mining needs steel. Leontief's framework quantified those mutual dependencies as a system of simultaneous equations, and the work earned him the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences in 1973.

The structure is identical to the cost problem, just scaled up. Each sector's total output equals what it sells to final demand plus what every other sector draws from it. Written in matrix form, the solution is the famous Leontief inverse:

total = own cost + cost drawn back from others
x = d + C·x   →   x = (I − C)⁻¹ · d

Swap "sectors" for "service departments" and "final demand" for "direct cost," and Leontief's economy-wide model becomes a single firm's cost ledger. The two-equation example above is just this inverse with two rows. From the 1960s onward, management accountants adopted the matrix formulation — which is why the reciprocal method is also called the algebraic or simultaneous-equations method.

A regulator's endorsement

In the 1970s the U.S. Cost Accounting Standards Board stated a preference for reciprocal allocation over the direct and step-down methods, on accuracy grounds. Notably, some defence contractors resisted — not because the method was wrong, but because the matrix algebra was considered too burdensome to implement. Accuracy was never the obstacle; tractability was.

05 — Solving it in practice

Two roads to the same number

There are two ways to actually compute the reciprocal answer, and they land in exactly the same place.

The algebraic route sets up the simultaneous equations and solves them directly — by hand for two departments, or by matrix inversion once there are several. It's exact and immediate, but it needs a linear-algebra step that many spreadsheets and planning tools don't express natively.

The iterative route — "repeated distribution" — sidesteps the algebra. You allocate every department's balance out by its percentages, including back to the other service departments; then you allocate what just landed back on them; and you repeat. Each pass, the amount still bouncing between service departments shrinks, until what remains is smaller than anyone cares about. It converges to the algebraic answer.

06 — In real systems

Where it bites — and why tooling struggles

Reciprocal apportionment turns up wherever shared functions genuinely serve one another and the resulting cost has to be defended: internal shared-services chargeback, regulated cost recovery, hospital and utility rate-setting, and the service-department layer beneath activity-based costing.

The recurring practical snag is the one the defence contractors hit fifty years ago, in modern dress. Most planning platforms and spreadsheets evaluate formulas in dependency order — and a reciprocal relationship is, by definition, a circular dependency. The tool refuses to compute A until B is known and B until A is known. So the reciprocal step is typically pushed outside the native formula engine: solved by iteration or matrix inversion in a separate routine, then the settled figures are written back in for the rest of the model to consume.

Seen in the wild

This is exactly the shape of the settlement stages in the falconbi cost-apportionment reference model. The Pool → Pool and Activity → Activity stages are reciprocal settlements; because the platform can't express a native circular formula, they're computed externally and read back from a settlement table — the write-back pattern described above, applied to a live model.
