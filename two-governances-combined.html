Two kinds of governance

The trade-off every EPM platform makes against itself — and the one thing that decides whether you can close it.

Ask whether an EPM platform can be "governed" and two people will argue past each other. The word hides two different things.

One word, two meanings

Control governs the form of a change. Proximity governs its meaning. Each is blind to what the other catches.

Control

Governs the form

Review, versioning, a diff, segregation of duties, lineage. Audits the pipes — no opinion on whether the number is right.

Proximity

Governs the meaning

The accountant builds the model directly. No translation layer, no gatekeeper — and no reviewer either.

Why you can't have both

The gate that gives you control is the same gate that costs you proximity. It's the pivot, not a feature you can add to either side.

The grid

Every EPM tool — TM1, Anaplan, Pigment — sits somewhere on that trade-off. None reach the corner with both.

Where the sweet spot is

Not the empty corner — you can't reach it. The target is a band just short of it: proximity with guardrails. Both sides move toward it from opposite directions.

The proof — two real bugs, one line

A double-counted account is structural — downstream checks catch it. A formula silently multiplying by twelve is evaluation — the hierarchy is spotless and the number is still wrong.

So the real question

Which failure can your organisation least afford?

Control corner fails as

The gatekeeper

Can operate the model. Can't tell when it's wrong.

Proximity corner fails as

The lone author

Understands it perfectly. Nobody checks the work.

An open ecosystem lets you buy back control; downstream checks claw some more. Neither fully closes the gap — but one thing decides whether a tool can close it at all.

The mechanism

Governance doesn't ship from the vendor. It gets built around the product. That needs an open API. No surface, no ecosystem, no fix.

TM1 proves it — TM1py, Pulse, Arc, Bedrock, custom IDEs, custom tooling. None of it IBM. All of it because the surface is open.

Five factors, two tools

Row 4 is the hinge. The API is what unlocks 3 and 5 — build it around the product. TM1 has the surface; Pigment doesn't. Both miss full marks on 4, for opposite reasons: TM1 has the API and weak proximity, Pigment has the proximity and no API.

Buildable, not bought

There's no mature Git-for-TM1 product today. But every primitive one would need already exists.

TM1's logic is text — rules and TI serialize to files. TM1py pulls them out; the REST API pushes them back. Git sits in the middle as the source of truth, and the whole PR loop is assemblable.

The limit: assemblable isn't shipped.

Noisy serialization to normalise, deploy ordering to respect, live-edit drift to fence off — and it takes someone fluent in TM1py, REST, Git and CI. The hybrid. Rare.

So TM1's tick on governance means buildable, not turnkey. Still worlds past Pigment — where the closed surface means it can't be attempted at all, by you or anyone but the vendor.

The catch

TM1's price is its face.

Dated UX, steep complexity, an implementation lift that pushes the very finance users it should empower back toward specialists. That's why it only half-ticks factors 1 and 2. The open surface makes governance buildable; the interface makes proximity hard-won.

The walled garden cuts both ways

Weak API hooks are a real limit — but the closed garden is also the draw. No open surface means no priesthood forms, no bespoke tooling to rot, no key-person risk around the one hybrid who understood the pipeline. For many teams that comfort is the right buy: the platform can't be governed deeply, and it also can't be broken deeply.

Pigment — closed by design

Limited by the weak API, but that same wall buys safety: no priesthood, no custom-tooling debt, no key-person risk. Comfort over control.

TM1 — open by design

The REST API offers extreme flexibility — for teams with the skills to wield it. Without them, the flexibility is just tech debt you now own.

The honest trade

TM1

You can build the sweet spot around it — if you can stomach the UX and the complexity.

Pigment

You're in the sweet spot on day one — and can't govern your way out of trouble.

Which failure you can least afford decides the corner you start in. The open API decides whether you can climb out of it — extreme flexibility for competent teams, at a cost; a walled garden's comfort for everyone else. Pick the failure you can live with, and the tool mostly follows.
