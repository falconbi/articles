Ragged Hierarchies in Pigment

Concepts, failure modes, and how to choose between the two implementation patterns.

Version: 1.0  ·  Author: James Lovering  ·  Last Updated: July 2026
Applies to: Any dimension list with a parent-child relationship of variable depth.
Read this first. It explains the problem and the choice. The two companion documents implement it.

Problem
Ragged hierarchies break level-based grouping. Shallow branches have no ancestor at deeper levels, so items silently vanish from BY aggregations — no error, totals short.
Root cause
Pigment’s formula language is a declarative DAG. No recursion, so hierarchy traversal must be unrolled to a fixed ceiling, and every level a node cannot reach is a blank.
The necessity
Every calculation grain needs a complete member set behind it. Both patterns satisfy this. They differ only in when completeness is built.
Balanced
Completeness built eagerly. Shallow nodes promote themselves at deeper levels. Nothing is ever blank. Safe by construction, hard to misuse.
Lean
Completeness built lazily. Level lists hold real members only. Safe as-built for leaf-grain work and all views. Level-grain metrics require a helper dimension — a rule that must be enforced.
Choose by
Who maintains the model. Balanced if you cannot guarantee everyone who touches it knows the rule. Lean if you can.

1. What a ragged hierarchy is

A ragged (unbalanced, variable-depth) hierarchy is one where branches terminate at different depths. Some leaves sit three levels down, others five. Almost every real organisational structure is ragged — a chart of accounts, a cost centre tree, a product hierarchy.

Marketing Depth 0 root ├── Digital Team Depth 1 has children → a real Level 2 node │ ├── Digital AMER Depth 2 leaf │ └── Digital EMEA Depth 2 leaf ├── AMER Marketing Depth 1 no children → a ragged leaf └── Brand Development Depth 1 no children → a ragged leaf

Digital AMER reaches Level 3. AMER Marketing stops at Level 2 — it is a leaf that never reaches the depth its siblings’ children do. That is the raggedness, and it is not a data quality problem. It is the shape of the business.

Three facts that govern everything downstream

Depth is measured from the root. It tells you how far down a node sits. It says nothing about what hangs below it.

Leaf-ness is a downward question. “Does anything name me as its parent?” No upward test can answer it. In the tree above, Digital Team and AMER Marketing are both Depth 1 — one is a real node, one is a ragged leaf. Depth cannot tell them apart. This distinction is the hinge on which the whole pattern turns.

Frame is the height of a hierarchy — the depth of its deepest branch, and therefore the number of levels it occupies. A five-level Balance Sheet has a frame of 5; a three-level P&L has a frame of 3. Frame is a property of the tree, where Depth is a property of a node within it. Distinguish it from the ceiling — the depth an implementation’s unrolled formulas support. A hierarchy with a frame of 3 sits comfortably under a ceiling of 7; tuning the ceiling means setting it to the frame, so that anything deeper fires as a data error.

Frame, and hierarchies of different heights

Frame matters as soon as two hierarchies of different heights share one set of level dimensions — a chart of accounts where the Balance Sheet runs five levels and the P&L runs three, both feeding Level 1 … Level 5.

Under the top-down numbering this library standardises on (Section 6), frame does not enter the placement arithmetic at all:

Level = Depth + 1  ·  independent of frame

Both roots sit at Level 1. Both second tiers sit at Level 2. The shallower hierarchy simply stops early — the P&L populates Levels 1–3 and contributes no members to Levels 4 or 5. Frame tells you which levels a hierarchy reaches, and sets the ceiling the unrolled cascade must cover. It does no work in the formulas.

Under bottom-up numbering, frame becomes load-bearing:

Level = Frame − Depth  ·  every placement formula must know the frame

Every level flag and every mapping property must then branch on which hierarchy an item belongs to before it can resolve a level, because the same Depth means different levels in each. That is a real and permanent cost, and it is a further argument for top-down — see Section 6.

2. Why Pigment forces the unroll

Pigment’s formula language is a declarative directed acyclic graph. Nodes are properties and metrics; an edge runs from A to B when B’s formula reads A. Nothing may depend on itself, however indirectly. You state what each property equals, not the order in which anything happens — the engine derives execution order by topological sort.

Three things fall out of that guarantee:

  • Incremental recalculation. Change one input, walk forward from that node. Only true dependents recompute.
  • Parallelism and caching. Independent subgraphs evaluate concurrently; each node is a pure function of its inputs, so results memoise safely.
  • Termination as a structural guarantee. No cycles means no infinite recalculation, no oscillation, no iteration limits. The model always settles, deterministically, in one pass.

The price is exact and unavoidable. Recursion is a cycle. “My root is my parent’s root” is f(x) = f(parent(x)), and any honest graph of that has an edge from the property to itself. So the entire class of walk-until-terminating algorithms is not slow or awkward — it is inexpressible. Hierarchy traversal, transitive closure, ancestor resolution at arbitrary depth: none of it can be written.

What you write instead is an unrolled cascade to a fixed ceiling, with a guard for data that exceeds it. That is not a hack. It is the honest encoding of a real constraint.

The circularity check is stricter than the mathematics

A self-referencing Root property — “inherit my parent’s Root; if my parent has none, my parent is the root” — is acyclic at the item level. A root item never consults its parent; a Depth-1 item reads only the root’s value; the chain terminates.

Pigment rejects it anyway, because the check operates on the property graph, not the item graph. It sees Root depending on Root and refuses. It cannot prove termination — nothing in the type system prevents your source data containing a parent cycle. The check is conservative, and conservative is correct: self-parented rows and reused keys are common in real GL extracts.

The permitted exception

Time-lagged self-reference is usually allowed. A closing balance at period n referencing the closing balance at n−1 looks circular but is not: the time offset makes it a strictly ordered chain, and the graph is over (property × period) rather than over properties alone. This is why balance-sheet roll-forwards work while hierarchy recursion does not. The engine permits self-reference along an axis it can guarantee is well-ordered. Structural depth has no such guarantee — the data supplies the order, and the data can lie.

3. Structure versus formula — the fork with TM1

In TM1, consolidation is a property of the dimension. An N-level rollup requires no expression at any depth, ever. Element weights carry the sign: revenue rolls into Net Profit at +1, expenses at −1, and the consolidation applies them automatically. The hierarchy is the arithmetic. MDX’s Ancestors() sits on top of a storage engine that already knows the tree.

In Pigment the dimension knows only that a property happens to point at the same list. Everything the edge would have carried — depth, level, ancestry, weight — must be reconstructed as data alongside it.

TM1Pigment
AggregationDimension structureMapping properties + BY
Depth / levelImplicit in the treeComputed property, unrolled cascade
Sign / weightElement weight on the edgeLeaf property, multiplied in the metric
Extending depthFreeOne more branch per formula

Anaplan carries the same constraint as Pigment, for the same reason. The comparison is not recursive versus not — it is structure-carries-meaning versus formulas-carry-meaning. Every artefact in these patterns exists because meaning had to leave the structure and become data.

Practical consequence: the sign convention

A P&L rolls naturally to Net Profit After Tax with no subtraction anywhere — provided revenue is positive and expenses negative. Source the debit/credit indicator from the GL (do not infer it from the branch; contra accounts such as Accumulated Depreciation sit under Assets but are credit-natured). Derive a numeric Sign on the leaf and multiply in the metric: [value] * Leaf.Sign. Signs at the leaf, then aggregate — the hierarchy does the rest. Models that skip this end up hand-writing every subtotal.

4. The failure mode

A ragged leaf has no ancestor at levels its branch never reaches. That level property is blank. What happens next is the single most important behaviour in this document.

A metric aggregated [BY: …Level N] silently excludes every item blank at Level N. No error. No null row. The total is simply short.

Empirically confirmed, July 2026. A four-item fixture — two items with a populated intermediate property, two blank, values totalling 28 — aggregated via [BY: …] to that property returned 13. The two blank items vanished. The control, with a fully-populated property, returned 28.

Views behave differently — and correctly

The same fixture, displayed in a view grouped by the same blank-bearing property, returned 28. Views retain blank-mapped items under a blank header and total completely. Charts configured from views inherit this.

This asymmetry is the trap. Every visual check passes. The hierarchy looks right, the drill-down looks right, the view totals are right. Only a metric taking a BY is wrong, and it is wrong silently.

The verification test

Ten minutes, and it settles any argument about whether a model is losing items.

// Control — a metric on the leaf dimension. Grand total = leaf count.
1

// One per level — should return the same total as the control.
'[control]'[BY: 'Leaf'.'Level 1']
'[control]'[BY: 'Leaf'.'Level 2']
'[control]'[BY: 'Leaf'.'Level 3']

Board the control and each level side by side. Every shortfall is the number of items that dropped. Two of these on one board — the control and the failing level — convert an unresolved design argument into a number that cannot be talked around.

5. The necessity, and the two answers

Strip away the implementations and one requirement remains:

Every calculation grain needs a complete member set behind it.

Both patterns satisfy this. They differ in when completeness is built and where the members live.

ConcernBalancedLean
Calculation safetyEvery level, by constructionLeaf / L1 / views safe as-built; level-grain metrics need a helper
When safety is paid forUp front — promotion, per-level importsOn demand — helper built when a consumer appears
Level list contentsReal + promoted membersReal members only; synthetics confined to a Calc helper
View appearanceShows promoted rowsClean — real members only
Leaf detectionNot required by the resolversRequired — every level gates on it
Hierarchy changesTouch multiple lists via importsTouch the Load List, re-import projections
Governance burdenNone ongoing — structure enforces safetyOne rule, enforced forever
Fails how, if misusedHard to misuseSilently, if the rule is breached

Why balanced needs no leaf detection

This is the cleanest way to see the difference. At a level’s own depth, a blank-based resolver must distinguish a real node (which belongs there) from a ragged leaf (which does not). Both sit at the same Depth. Only a downward child-count separates them — so every level resolver must be gated on it.

Promotion never makes that distinction. “At your own depth, return yourself” is correct for both: a real node returns itself legitimately, and a leaf returning itself is the intended promotion. The gate disappears, and with it an entire property.

The lean build rule — non-negotiable

Any metric dimensioned by a level, or using BY into a level, uses a Calc helper dimension: that level’s real members plus one synthetic member per root, so the mapping is never blank. Everything the helper needs already exists in the Load List resolvers — it is an import and one property, buildable at any time, zero rework. That is what makes deferral legitimate. Knowing you needed one is the hard part, and the reason this rule must be written into the model’s design notes rather than held in a maintainer’s head.

6. Level numbering — top-down, leaf position-free

Number levels from the root. Level 1 is the root. Level 2 is the tier below it. Name the metric-facing leaf dimension without a position at all — Cost Centre, never Cost Centre L3.

Two reasons, and they reinforce each other.

Stability. Hierarchies deepen at the bottom. You do not discover a new root above Assets; you discover that a leaf now has children. Under top-down numbering the root stays Level 1 forever, Level 2 stays Level 2, and the new level appends below. Nothing above it moves.

Intelligibility. Everyone reads a tree from the top. Level 1 is Assets, and no one needs to be told.

The bottom-up variant, and why it fails

A bottom-up scheme — leaf = L0, L1 = one above the leaf, counting upward — appears to protect the leaf dimension from renaming when the hierarchy deepens. It does not.

  • It shifts every other label. New levels appear at the leaf end, so every existing leaf becomes L1, every L1 becomes L2, and every level dimension needs renaming. One rename avoided; a cascading rename introduced.
  • The same label means different tiers. Where two hierarchies of different heights share level dimensions, “Level 1” sits at one depth in the first and another in the second. The roots land on different level numbers.
  • Frame becomes load-bearing. Placement is Level = Frame − Depth rather than Level = Depth + 1, so every level flag and every mapping property must first branch on which hierarchy an item belongs to. Under top-down, frame never enters a formula (Section 1).
  • The leaf name is better protected by not encoding position at all. Which costs nothing.

The underlying fact: the root end is pinned; the leaf end is free. Raggedness lives there, new levels appear there, and labels anchored there move whenever the data does. Anchor at the root and everything above stays still.

7. Leaf detection — two idioms

The downward question: does anything name me as its parent?

MATCH — a List Property

// Is_Leaf — nobody references this item's key as their Parent_ID.
IF(ISBLANK(MATCH('Load'.Code, 'Load'.Parent_ID)), TRUE)

Self-contained on the list, no extra object. Requires a text Parent_ID (derived as Parent.Code), because MATCH cannot compare a dimension reference against text.

BY COUNT — a staging metric

// Has_Children — TRUE if any item names this item as its parent.
'Load'.Internal_ID [BY COUNT: 'Load'.Parent_Internal_ID] > 0

// Is_Leaf — the inverse. NOT on a blank returns TRUE; no guard needed.
NOT 'Load'.'Has_Children'

BY COUNT cannot be expressed as a List Property — it must be a metric dimensioned by the Load List, sitting beside it and feeding back onto its classification. It groups on the dimension reference directly, in a single pass.

Choose by size

MATCH is the cleaner default for small and moderate lists — one property, no metric. BY COUNT is the proven idiom at volume: it groups once, where MATCH searches a column per row. MATCH has not been benchmarked on large dimensions; test before committing on a list of tens of thousands of items.

8. Data preconditions

Structural faults in the source break a self-referencing dimension before any formula runs. Check before building — all of these have been seen in production GL extracts:

  • Non-unique keys. One code assigned to two different items. The identifier is rejected and the parent chain cannot resolve. Fix at source. Never fabricate a substitute code, and never break the source numbering scheme to force uniqueness.
  • Self-parented rows. Parent = self. A circular dependency error on load.
  • Duplicate rows under different parents. The same item under two parents — an asset in one row, a liability in another. This is an ownership question, not a technical one, and needs a human answer.
  • A source level column that disagrees with the parent chain. Trust computed Depth, never a source Level label. They diverge more often than expected, and the label is the one that lies.
  • Repeated names. A group and its posting account both called “Goodwill” is normal and correct. Key on the code, never the name.

9. Which pattern

The question is not which is better

It is: do you trust everyone who will ever touch this model to know the rule?

Lean where a disciplined maintainer controls the model, clean member-only display matters, and the build rule can be relied upon. You get cheap hierarchy changes, no synthetic clutter, and you pay for completeness only when a consumer appears.

Balanced where data-loss safety must be guaranteed regardless of who touches the model next. A client model, handed to a team, after the author has left. Balanced makes the failure impossible rather than merely documented.

The two are the same architecture with completeness built lazily versus eagerly. Neither is a compromise; both are correct. The choice is about governance, not about modelling.

10. A note on cost

Six kinds of artefact — staging list, depth property, level flags, level dimensions, imports, mapping properties — reproduce what a dimension in TM1 does for free. It is a great deal of scaffolding, and the instinct that it ought to be simpler is correct.

But note what remains once Depth exists. Every artefact is deterministic given the flat list, the key, the parent, the ceiling, and the fill policy. The unrolling becomes mechanical: dispatch on a known depth, walk a known distance. Formula length grows linearly with depth — verbose, never structural.

Contrast a three-role classification (leaf / intermediate / root), which is depth-agnostic but expresses only three states. It is sufficient for a three-level dimension and cannot distinguish Level 1 from Level 4. Extending it means redesigning the classification; extending a depth-based scheme means adding a branch.

Depth looks redundant in a three-level role-based design and is essential in an N-level depth-based one. That single property marks the fork between “works to three” and “works to any depth.”
