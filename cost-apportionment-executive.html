The problem it solves

From a raw ledger to a number you can defend

Most of what an organisation spends is shared. Facilities, IT, HR, finance and other support functions don't belong to any single service — yet their cost has to land somewhere if leaders are to know what each service line truly costs to run.

Done in spreadsheets, that attribution is slow, hard to audit, and easy to argue with. This model replaces it with a single, transparent engine: overhead enters as unstructured ledger balances and leaves as a fully reconciled cost per service line, with every step visible and every dollar accounted for.

Overhead goes in as a ledger. It comes out as a defensible cost per service.
How it works

The cost journey, in five steps

Cost moves through five sequential steps. Two of them are settlements — moments where support functions that serve one another square up their mutual costs before the model moves on, so no one function is left carrying more or less than its fair share.

1

Into cost pools

Shared overhead in the ledger is grouped into cost pools and spread using fair-share drivers — measures like headcount, floor space or FTE that reflect who actually consumes the cost. Costs that clearly belong to a single service line are set aside here and rejoin at the end.

1b

Pools settle with each other

Some support pools serve other support pools — IT supports HR, HR supports IT. This step settles those mutual costs so each pool ends up carrying the right total before anything moves downstream.

Settlement step
2

Into activities

Settled pool costs flow to the activities the organisation actually performs — again split by drivers that reflect real usage.

2b

Activities settle with each other

The same settlement logic, one level down: activities that support one another square up their mutual costs before the final allocation.

Settlement step
3

Onto service lines

Activity costs land on the service lines that draw on them. The direct costs set aside in step 1 rejoin here — giving a single, complete figure: total cost per service line.

Why it can be trusted

Reconciled at every step, not just at the end

The value of a cost allocation is only as good as the confidence you can place in it. This model is built so that no money is quietly lost or created as cost moves through the chain — and so that a failure anywhere is caught before results are used.

At every stage the model confirms that what went in equals what came out. The total overhead entering the chain must equal the total landing on service lines — checked at the pool level, the activity level, and again on the final result. Each check reports a plain pass or fail with the exact variance, so there is never any doubt about whether the numbers tie.

The validation gate

Before a set of results is released, every check is consolidated onto a single sign-off dashboard. It is a deliberate control point: the run proceeds only when everything reads green. This is what turns a calculation into an attestable result.

Where it stands

Current status

The model is complete and reconciling across all five stages. There is one known item on the roadmap, called out here in the interest of a straight picture.

Open item · settlement automation

The two settlement steps (pools and activities squaring up mutual costs) are designed to run automatically once triggered. That automation is not yet switched on, so today those settlements are triggered manually. It does not affect the accuracy of the result — only the amount of hands-on effort to produce a run.

What it's built on

A connected model, not a spreadsheet

The engine is built in Pigment, a cloud-native planning platform. That choice buys three things that matter at the executive level:

  • One source of truth. The whole allocation lives in a single connected model rather than a chain of linked spreadsheets, so there is one version of the numbers, not a dozen.
  • Runs across planning cycles. The same engine handles Actual, Budget and Forecast, so cost-per-service can be compared consistently across what happened, what was planned, and what's expected.
  • Open by design. The methodology is published in full — the logic is inspectable, not a black box, which is exactly what an auditor or a board wants to see behind a cost number.
